Dream Weaver — Questions for Doug
Before Any Public Launch
These are not technical questions. They are clinical, ethical, and philosophical questions that require the perspective of someone who has actually done this work and understands what is at stake when depth psychological tools are put in the hands of people without analytical containers.

1. The Guide Requirement
The app currently requires users to name a human guide before accessing the full practice tools. The assumption is that serious depth work requires a human witness.
Questions:

Is this the right requirement, or is it too restrictive? Are there people doing legitimate solo inner work who would benefit from this app without a guide?
What constitutes a sufficient guide? The app currently accepts any analyst, therapist, or spiritual director. Should there be more specificity about what kind of training or experience the guide should have?
What is your liability concern, if any, about someone using this app without adequate human support?
Should the app include a crisis resource or mental health disclaimer anywhere? At what point does inner work become something that needs clinical intervention rather than analytical support?


2. The AI Analysis — Clinical Ethics
The app generates Jungian dream analysis using Claude (Opus model). The analysis reads the dream in the context of the dreamer's recent dream history and attempts to identify archetypes, symbols, complexes, and the four-part dramatic structure (exposition, development, peripeteia, lysis).
The core concern:
Jung never analyzed a dream without first gathering the dreamer's own associations. The app currently skips this step — or rather, offers it as optional (the dreamer associations field) but the AI analysis doesn't genuinely wait for or respond to those associations. It appends them to the prompt but generates analysis regardless.
Questions:

Is AI-generated dream analysis without prior association-gathering analytically sound, or does it risk imposing meaning rather than discovering it?
Should the analysis be more dialogical — asking the dreamer questions rather than delivering conclusions? What would that look like?
How much does the analysis feel like genuine Jungian amplification versus something closer to interpretation? Would you be comfortable with a patient reading this kind of analysis between sessions?
The app frames AI insights as "invitations, not verdicts." Is that framing sufficient, or does the confidence of a well-written AI analysis override the disclaimer in practice?
Should there be a resonance check built into every analysis — a simple prompt asking the dreamer how much the analysis lands before they internalize it?


3. Active Imagination
The app has an Active Imagination feature where the dreamer writes both sides of the dialogue themselves — both the ego voice and the figure's voice. The AI prepares the session (drawing from the dream archive to profile the figure) and reflects on the session afterward (reading the dialogue the dreamer wrote).
The AI does NOT voice the figure. We tested this approach and rejected it after finding that AI-voiced figures confabulate, impose meaning, and produce responses that felt performative rather than genuine.
Questions:

Is the writing-both-sides approach a legitimate form of active imagination, or does it risk the ego simply writing what it wants to hear?
What are Jung's own guidelines for knowing whether it is the unconscious speaking versus the ego? The app currently includes educational notes on this distinction — are they adequate?
The preparation panel provides a suggested opening line and questions to hold. Is this too much scaffolding, or is it genuinely helpful for someone new to the practice?
The app offers an AI "analyst's reflection" on the completed session. Is this appropriate? Does it risk short-circuiting the dreamer's own integration of the experience?
Is there a risk that the writing practice substitutes for actual active imagination (which Jung conducted in a more embodied, imaginal state) rather than supporting it?


4. Shadow Work
The app identifies shadow material in dreams using AI — surfacing shadow figures, projected qualities, and a reflection prompt. The dreamer can record shadow encounters (dream-based, projection-based, or reaction-based) and track their integration status over time.
Questions:

Is AI-assisted shadow identification clinically appropriate? Is there a risk of the AI labeling something as shadow material that isn't, or missing shadow content that is present but subtle?
The app uses language like "projected qualities" and "where do you carry this yourself?" — essentially walking the dreamer through shadow recognition independently. Is this safe without a guide present?
Shadow work without adequate container can destabilize people. What safeguards should be in place? Is the guide requirement sufficient?
The integration status options are: Active, Becoming conscious, Metabolizing, Integrated. Does this schema feel clinically accurate to how shadow integration actually progresses?
Is there anything about the way shadow material is presented that risks shaming the dreamer, even inadvertently?


5. The Individuation Narrative
The app generates a longitudinal narrative of the dreamer's individuation journey using the full dream archive. The narrative is structured in chapters, each with a theme, a core question, dream references, and a closing invitation.
You have seen a version of this — it analyzed fifteen years of dream material and produced something that felt analytically substantial.
Questions:

How does the narrative read to you analytically? Does it feel like genuine Jungian reflection or does it feel like sophisticated pattern-matching that mimics depth without having it?
Is there anything in the narrative that concerns you clinically — either in what it says or in how it frames the journey?
The narrative is generated periodically and updated incrementally as new dreams are added. Is this an appropriate rhythm, or should there be more deliberate timing around when someone reads a narrative of their own journey?
Should the narrative always be read with a guide present, or is it safe to read independently?


6. The Embodiment Principle
The app is built around the principle that inner work must land in outer life. Every dream analysis ends with an embodiment prompt — a question pointing toward concrete action in the physical world this week. There is a weekly check-in asking "did anything shift?"
Questions:

Does the embodiment prompt approach feel aligned with how you work with patients? Are the prompts the right kind of questions — concrete enough to be actionable, open enough to not be prescriptive?
Is there a risk that the embodiment prompts become another thing to track and feel guilty about, rather than a genuine invitation toward life?
The app includes a "pause gate" — a gentle intervention when it detects compulsive recording patterns. Does this feel appropriate? Is there a better way to address the bypassing tendency?


7. Plant Medicine and Altered State Material
A future feature would allow dreamers to record and analyze material from plant medicine journeys, hypnosis sessions, breathwork experiences, and other altered states alongside nighttime dreams.
Questions:

Should plant medicine material be analyzed using the same Jungian framework as dreams, or does it require a different analytical approach?
Are there clinical reasons NOT to include plant medicine material in this kind of app?
What is your view on the relationship between plant medicine experiences and the individuation process?
Should this feature wait until the app has been through proper clinical review?


8. The Hero's Journey and Heroine's Journey
A future feature would allow dreamers to optionally map their journey against Campbell's Hero's Journey or Maureen Murdock's Heroine's Journey.
Questions:

Do you find these frameworks clinically useful in your work? Do they illuminate or do they impose?
Is the Heroine's Journey (Murdock) a framework you work with? Are there other feminine frameworks that would be more appropriate?
Should these be presented as optional lenses or not included at all?


9. The Guide Access System
A future feature would allow the dreamer to share their material with their guide through a secure read-only link. The guide could leave annotations on specific dreams and a general reflection. This happens asynchronously between sessions or on a shared screen during sessions.
Questions:

Would you actually use this? Be honest — would having access to a patient's dream journal between sessions change how you work, or would it be more material than is useful?
What would you want to see as a guide? What would you not want to see?
Are there clinical reasons a guide should NOT have access to raw dream material outside the session container?
The annotation feature allows the guide to leave notes on specific passages. Does this feel appropriate, or does it risk the guide's interpretation colonizing the dreamer's own process?
Would you want to see the dreamer's private notes and analyst session notes, or only the dreams themselves?


10. Opening to Other Users
If this app is eventually made available to others:
Questions:

What kind of vetting or onboarding should be required before someone accesses the full depth of the tools?
Should there be different tiers — a lighter version for people new to inner work, and the full version for people already in analytical work?
What is your view on the liability and ethical responsibility of offering depth psychological tools outside a clinical context?
Would you be willing to be named as a clinical consultant or advisor? What would that require from you?
Are there other Jungian analysts or depth psychologists you think should review this before it goes public?
What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable recommending this app to a patient?


11. The Name and Framing
The app is currently called Dream Weaver — a name that honors the energy you named, the intelligence that sends dreams. The broader platform is called The Becoming.
Questions:

Does the name Dream Weaver feel appropriate for this use? Any concerns about how it might be perceived?
The onboarding tells new users: "This will help you go down." Is that framing honest without being alarming?
The app describes itself as "for people already in the fire." Is this the right positioning, or does it risk excluding people who are ready for this work but don't yet know it?
